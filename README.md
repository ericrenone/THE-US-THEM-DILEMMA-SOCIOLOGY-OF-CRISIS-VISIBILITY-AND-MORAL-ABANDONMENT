## PART I: THE TEMPORAL INVERSION — WHY CRISES ARE SPATIAL, NOT BIOLOGICAL

### A. The 2014 Ebola Timeline: Before and After "Us"

The 2014–2016 Ebola outbreak provides a canonical case study in how institutional resource allocation is not triggered by disease burden, but by **whether the disease threatens the in-group**.

**Initial Spread:**

- No emergency funding legislation

- Global commitments: <$200 million

- Narrative frame: "Humanitarian crisis" (NGO domain, not government priority)

**The Event:**
- September 28, 2014: First confirmed Ebola case in the United States (Dallas, Texas)

- Widespread public alarm and media saturation ("It was all Ebola, all the time")

**Total U.S. commitment by December 2014:** >$4 billion (single country)
**Global commitment increase:** 500%+ overnight

**The Temporal Inversion:**
- **10 months after outbreak officially confirmed:** No emergency designation
- **1 month after first U.S. case:** Full mobilization

**Key Data Point:** The U.S. committed more resources to 3 Ebola cases in the USA than to 2,000+ cases across Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone until the Dallas case activated the boundary alarm.

## PART II: SYMBOLIC BOUNDARIES AND THE GEOGRAPHY OF MORAL VALUE

### A. Lamont's Framework: How Moral Boundaries Create and Sustain Inequality

Sociologist Michèle Lamont's work on **symbolic boundaries** provides the theoretical architecture for understanding why "them" deaths and "us" deaths trigger fundamentally different capital allocations.

#### **What Are Symbolic Boundaries?**

Symbolic boundaries are **the distinctions people draw** to define who belongs to their group (in-group) and who does not (out-group). These are not natural. They are socially constructed and continuously reinforced through:

1. **Moral boundaries** — judgments of who is morally worthy
2. **Cultural boundaries** — distinctions in taste, lifestyle, education
3. **Socioeconomic boundaries** — distinctions in wealth, power, occupational status

**Key Finding from Lamont's Research:**
"Moral values are chief among the ways we define our groups. We use these beliefs to delimit purported moral boundaries as symbolic treatments that are experienced as fundamental to in-groups. Such moral criteria serve to maintain and create institutionalized social differences."

#### **Application to Ebola vs. Space Funding**

**The Symbolic Boundary in Crisis Response:**

| Dimension | "Us" (In-Group) | "Them" (Out-Group) | How Boundaries Reinforce Inequality |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Geography** | North America, Western Europe | Sub-Saharan Africa | Geographic distance = moral distance |
| **Wealth** | $50K–$200K+ annual income | <$2,000 annual income | Rich lives "count" more |
| **Healthcare Access** | Isolation wards, ICUs, vaccines | Supportive care only, no vaccines | Some lives are "savable," others not |
| **Institutional Power** | OECD, G7, WHO board seats | NGO-dependent, weak state capacity | Some voices shape policy; others don't |
| **Moral Value** | "Civilization," "development," "advanced" | "Primitive," "disease-prone," "tribal" | Stereotypes justify differential treatment |

**The Boundary Mechanism:** When an Ebola case appears in Dallas (crossing the geographic boundary from "them" to "us"), institutional capital mobilizes overnight. This is not because disease biology changed. It is because the **moral boundary shifted**.

Dallas Ebola patient Thomas Eric Duncan was a Liberian traveler. But the moment he landed in Dallas, he became part of "us." His death demanded billions in institutional response. The thousands dying in Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone remained "them" until the spatial boundary was breached.

#### **Historical Proof: Colonial Medicine as Boundary Work**

Lamont's framework applies precisely to the history of infectious disease response:

**Sleeping Sickness (Trypanosomiasis, 1900s-1950s):**
- Affected: ~10 million Africans during colonial rule
- Deaths: Millions
- Colonial response: Minimal until the disease threatened European settlers
- Mechanism: African deaths were in the "them" category; they were colonized peoples
- Once disease threatened Europeans (the in-group): Emergency research, treatment, infrastructure

**The Pattern:** Resources flow toward threats to the in-group. Out-group suffering is classified as "humanitarian" (charity domain, not government priority).

1. Favor in-group members (in-group favoritism)

## PART IV: THE IDENTIFIABLE VICTIM EFFECT AND PSYCHIC NUMBING

### A. Why One American Matters More Than 837 Africans

The **Identifiable Victim Effect** (IVE) is a well-established psychological phenomenon that explains why abstract statistics ("837 cases in DRC") trigger zero institutional response, while "Thomas Eric Duncan in Dallas" triggers $6 billion.

#### **The Mechanism**

**Research Finding (Small et al., 2007):**
People respond more strongly to identifiable, named victims than to statistical victims **even when the statistical victims are numerically equivalent and equally deserving**.

**Example (Jessica McClure, 1987):**
- An 18-month-old girl falls into an abandoned well in Texas
- Rescue operation watched live on television
- Public donations: >$700,000
- Government resources: Unconstrained (no cost-benefit analysis applied)

**Contrast:**
- Same year, same country: Preventable deaths from malnutrition, diarrheal disease, etc. = thousands
- Government resources: Minimal
- Public donations: Negligible

**Why This Happens:**
Identifiable victims activate **emotional centers** in the human brain (medial cingulate cortex, insula) that statistical victims do not. Psychologist Paul Slovic calls this **"psychic numbing"** — as the number of victims rises, emotional impact *declines*.

**The Equation:**
- 1 identified victim (with name, face, story) = Maximum emotional response
- 10 identified victims = Less emotional response (paradoxically)
- 837 statistical victims = Zero emotional response (beyond initial shock)
- 10+ million statistical victims (HIV in sub-Saharan Africa, 1990s) = Completely numb (institutional and public indifference for years)

#### **Application to Ebola and Bundibugyo**

**Thomas Eric Duncan (Dallas, 2014):**
- Name: ✓ Identified
- Face: ✓ Photographed extensively
- Story: ✓ Liberian traveling to reunite with fiancée
- Emotional trigger: **Maximum**
- Institutional response: **$6.18 billion**

**837 Bundibugyo patients (DRC/Uganda, 2026):**
- Names: ✗ Majority unidentified in Western media
- Faces: ✗ Depersonalized as "victims" or "patients"
- Stories: ✗ Reduced to statistics ("837 confirmed cases, 232 deaths")
- Emotional trigger: **Minimal**
- Institutional response: **$360 million (6 months)**

**The Paradox:** 837 identifiable people in a documentary or news campaign would likely trigger 10x more funding than the same 837 people reported as statistics. But the in-group boundary makes documentation of Bundibugyo patients a low media priority. Why film African suffering when the disease hasn't reached Dallas?

## PART V: MORAL DISENGAGEMENT AND THE NORMALIZATION OF ABANDONMENT

### A. Bandura's Framework: How We Justify Letting People Die

Psychologist Albert Bandura's theory of **Moral Disengagement** explains how individuals and institutions can normalize the abandonment of out-group members.

#### **Four Mechanisms of Moral Disengagement**

1. **Moral Justification**
   - Reframing harmful action as serving worthy purposes
   - Example: "Space exploration benefits humanity" (ignoring that specific humans in DRC die of curable disease)

2. **Euphemistic Labeling**
   - Using sanitized language to obscure harm
   - Example: Calling Bundibugyo "a humanitarian issue" rather than "institutional abandonment of a curable disease"
   - Effect: Shifts responsibility from government to NGOs (charity domain)

3. **Advantageous Comparison**
   - Comparing harmful action to worse alternatives
   - Example: "We're funding Ebola response more than we are malaria" (ignoring that both are underfunded relative to need)

4. **Displacement of Responsibility**
   - Shifting responsibility to other agents
   - Example: "The WHO should handle this" (while G7 handles space technology, ignoring that G7 sets institutional priorities)

#### **Application to the Bundibugyo Case (June 2026)**

**Mechanism 1: Moral Justification**
- "SpaceX is advancing human knowledge and economic opportunity"
- "Space technology benefits all humanity through communications and GPS"
- Unspoken: "Therefore, it's acceptable that 837 Africans die of a vaccine-preventable disease"

**Mechanism 2: Euphemistic Labeling**
- Bundibugyo response is framed as "WHO's responsibility" (not institutional priority)
- SpaceX IPO is framed as "market-driven innovation" (institutional priority)
- Effect: The deaths in DRC become *humanitarian issues*; space becomes *economic priority*

**Mechanism 3: Advantageous Comparison**
- "We've allocated $270 million to Ebola response overall"
- Ignored: This is $238 less per confirmed case than SpaceX receives per IPO dollar
- Effect: The allocation appears reasonable in comparison to "worse options"

**Mechanism 4: Displacement of Responsibility**
- G7 summit avoids Bundibugyo agenda (responsibility displaced to WHO)
- WHO lacks resources (responsibility bounces back to governments)
- Governments cite budget constraints (responsibility bounces to markets)
- Markets cite profit motive (responsibility returns to governments)
- Result: **No one is responsible; everyone claims constraints they didn't create**

## PART VII: THE INSTITUTIONAL MACHINERY OF INDIFFERENCE

### A. How Institutions Formalize the In-Group/Out-Group Boundary

Institutions do not discriminate accidentally. They **systematize** in-group bias through resource allocation structures that normalize differential valuation of lives.

#### **Mechanism 1: The DALY Framework and Spatial Valuation**

The WHO uses **Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs)** to measure disease burden and allocate resources.

**DALY calculation:**
- Years of life lost (YLL) + Years lived with disability (YLD)
- Cost-effectiveness threshold: <$150,000 per DALY averted (wealthy nations)

**The Catch:**
- This threshold is applied **differently** by income level

**Actual funding allocations (2026):**

| Intervention | Location | Cost per DALY | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cancer treatment | USA | $100K–$1M per DALY | Funded extensively |
| Dialysis | USA | $50K/year (multiple DALYs) | Funded universally |
| Ebola vaccine | DRC/Uganda | $1K–$10K per DALY | Underfunded until crisis |
| Malaria prevention | Sub-Saharan Africa | $500–$5K per DALY | Chronically underfunded |

**The Pattern:** The same cost-effectiveness threshold ($150K/DALY) is applied inconsistently. When "us" dies, we spend $1M per DALY. When "them" dies, we spend $1K per DALY.

**The Mechanism:** The formalization of this inequality through economic metrics makes it seem **rational** rather than **racial**.
